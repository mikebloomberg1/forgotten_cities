America’s 179 Forgotten Cities

In 2007, urban scholars Lorlene Hoyt and André Leroux convened a set of conversations at MIT’s Department of Urban Studies and Planning about the country’s “Forgotten Cities.” They defined these places as old (with at least 5,000 residents in 1880), small (between 15,000 and 150,000 residents), and poor (a median household income below $35,000 at the time). Their research identified 150 such cities across the United States.

In 2024, I recreated and updated that list using the same population criteria (population in 1880 and in 2022) and an income threshold of 17% or more below the national median household income, matching the relative standard used in the original analysis. This update finds 179 Forgotten Cities today.

You can read the original 2007 paper here: Hoyt & Leroux (2007) .

The updated 2024 dataset was used by journalist Alec MacGillis in his New York Times/ProPublica analysis on the political dynamics of America’s small cities.
